Building in public as an engineer who wants better work

February 10, 20264 min readBy Harman Kamboj
CareerBuilding in public

Building in public as an engineer gets dismissed as influencer behavior, and I understand why. Most of what shows up under that banner is hype, follower counts, and people optimizing for engagement instead of truth. But there is a quieter version that has nothing to do with going viral and everything to do with getting better work, sharper thinking, and conversations with people you actually want to know. That version is worth defending.

What building in public is not

It is not posting a milestone every hour. It is not pretending every day is a breakthrough. It is not turning your craft into a content treadmill where the work exists to feed the posts. When the audience becomes the point, the work suffers, and people can smell that. I have unfollowed plenty of accounts that started as builders and slowly became advertisements for themselves.

The healthy version is closer to working with the garage door open. You share real progress, real problems, and the occasional dead end, because that is what your work actually looks like. The honesty is the whole value. Nobody learns anything from a highlight reel, and nobody trusts one either.

Why it pays off for engineers

The career math is simple. Opportunities come from people who have seen your thinking, not just your title. When you write about a hard problem you solved, you are leaving a trail that the right person can find. I have had conversations start because someone read how I reasoned through a system, which is a far better filter than a cold application that competes on keywords.

There is also a selfish benefit that has nothing to do with anyone else reading it. Explaining your work forces you to understand it. I have caught my own muddy thinking more times than I can count by trying to write a clear paragraph about a decision. If you cannot explain why you chose an approach, you probably do not understand it as well as you assumed.

The compounding effect is the part people underrate. A job application is gone the moment a role is filled. A post sits there and keeps working for years, getting found by people you will never meet through a search you will never see. I have had a piece written long ago surface in a conversation much later, doing useful work while I was busy with something else entirely. That kind of quiet return is rare and it costs only an hour of honest writing.

  • Writing exposes the gaps in your own reasoning
  • A public trail compounds while job applications expire
  • People hire thinking they have already seen, not promises

What is actually worth sharing

You do not need to leak proprietary code or breach anyone's trust to do this well. I share the shape of problems, the tradeoffs, the general lessons, never confidential specifics from client work. There is endless material in the how and the why that gives away nothing sensitive. A post about handling a hard category of bug is useful to everyone and harmful to no one.

Across years of shipping indexers, swap interfaces, bridges and a pile of games, the most valuable things to write about turned out to be the boring ones. How I think about a tradeoff. Why I picked one tool over another. What I would do differently. Nobody needs another launch announcement, but a genuine reasoning trace is rare and people remember it.

Doing it without becoming insufferable

The line between sharing and self-promotion is real, and you cross it the moment the work becomes an excuse to talk about yourself. I try to keep the focus on the problem and let any credit be a side effect. If a post would be useful to a stranger who has no idea who I am, it is probably fine. If it only makes sense as a flex, it goes in the bin.

Consistency matters more than frequency. One honest post a month for two years builds more than a daily blitz that burns out in March. This is a long game played by people who are still building when the hype crowd has moved on, and the audience that sticks around for the real thing is exactly the audience worth having. Show your work, keep it true, and let the rest take care of itself.
