Building a developer community that actually sticks

February 28, 20265 min readBy Harman Kamboj
Game devBuilding in public

Most developer communities die not because the topic was wrong but because the founder got bored around month three. I have run a game-dev community and helped organize jams long enough to watch that pattern repeat in other groups, and the ones that survive share a few unglamorous habits. Building a developer community is less about clever growth tactics and more about being the person who reliably shows up when nobody else feels like it.

The first hundred members are not a marketing problem

There is a temptation to chase numbers. Post everywhere, run a giveaway, get a thousand sign-ups, feel great for a week. Then the channel goes silent because none of those people had a reason to come back. Early on, a community is not a size, it is a handful of real relationships. Ten people who know each other and ship things together will outlast a thousand lurkers every time.

So I optimize for depth before width. I learn names. I remember what someone is working on and ask about it next week. That sounds soft and obvious, but it is the actual engine. People stay where they feel seen, and they leave where they feel like a metric.

Lower the bar to contribute, on purpose

The single biggest killer of momentum is a community where contributing feels intimidating. If the only people who post are five experts dropping polished work, everyone else assumes they have nothing worth sharing and goes quiet. You have to actively make small contributions feel welcome.

A few things that work for me:

  • Celebrate the ugly first build louder than the finished masterpiece, so people share work in progress
  • Ask beginner questions yourself in public, which gives everyone permission to not know things
  • Create low-stakes recurring rituals, like a weekly what-are-you-working-on thread, that need zero courage to join
  • Reply fast and warmly to first-time posters, because the first reply someone gets decides whether they ever post again

None of that scales in the spreadsheet sense, and that is fine. Community is not supposed to scale like a CDN.

Events give people a reason and a deadline

Game jams taught me how powerful a shared deadline is. A community that only chats slowly drifts. A community that builds something together on a clock comes alive. The jam is not really about the games, it is about a few dozen people all in the same boat at the same time, helping each other, complaining about scope together, and crossing a finish line on the same weekend.

You do not need a full jam. A monthly challenge, a build-along, a show-and-tell call, any recurring event with a date attached creates the rhythm. The rhythm is what turns a Discord server from a graveyard into a place people open on purpose. Without events, the median community decays into a feed of links nobody reads.

Moderation is mostly setting the tone, not banning people

Everyone worries about toxic members and rules. In my experience the bigger lever is the tone the founders set in the first weeks, because new members copy whatever they see. If the early posts are generous and curious, the culture trends generous and curious. If the early posts are snark and one-upmanship, you have built a place where beginners feel stupid, and beginners are most of your future.

Hard rules still matter, and you should write the obvious ones down and enforce them without drama. But ninety percent of culture is modeled, not enforced. The way you respond to a clumsy question teaches more than any pinned code of conduct.

Why founders burn out, and how to not

The quiet truth is that running a community is unpaid emotional labor, and the founder usually does most of it alone until they crack. The fix is to stop being the only one who carries it. Hand real ownership to a few committed members early, even before you feel ready, because a community with three caretakers survives a bad month and a community with one does not.

I also gave myself permission to be less present sometimes. A community that only works when the founder is online every day is fragile. The healthy version keeps humming when you take a week off, because the rituals and the relationships do the work, not your constant attention. Build toward that from the start and you will still be running it in three years instead of abandoning it in three months.

What sticking actually looks like

A community that sticks is boring in the best way. Same people show up, new people trickle in and feel at home fast, projects get shipped, and the place has inside jokes. There is no growth hack behind it. There is just a long stretch of someone caring consistently, lowering the bar, running the events, and modeling the tone, week after week, until it becomes a thing bigger than any one person.
